  1. Note that \(c(s,1) = λ\), which is a constant and \(c(\cdot,2) = [0,1,5]\), which is increasing. Thus (C1) is satisfied.

    Now we consider stochastic monotonicity of the transition matrices. \(P(1) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\). Since all rows of \(P(1)\) are identical, it is trivially, stochsatic monotone.

    \(P(2) = \begin{bmatrix} 1-θ & θ & 0 \\ 0 & 1-θ & θ \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\). By construction, the CDF of the first row lies below the CDF of the second row, which lies below the CDF of the third row. Hence, the matrix is stochastic monotone. Thus, condition (C2) is satisfied.

    Now we consider sub-modularity of the cost. Consider \(c(s,2) - c(s,1)\). Since \(c(s,1)\) is a constant and \(c(s,2)\) is weakly increasing in \(s\), this implies that \(c(s,2) - c(s,1)\) is weakly increasing in \(s\). Hence, the cost function is sub-modular. Thus, condition (C4a) is satisfied.

    Now, we consider \(H(s'|s,a) = 1 - \sum_{z \le s'} P_{sz}(a)\). We will think of \(H(a)\) as a matrix (similar to the notation for the transition matrix \(P(a)\)). Note that, \[ H(1) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad H(2) = \begin{bmatrix} θ & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & θ & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \] For every fixed value of \(s'\), \(H(s'|s,2) - H(s'|s,1) = H(s'|s,2)\) is increasing in \(s\). Hence \(H(s'|s,a)\) is supermodular in \((s,a)\). Thus, condition (C4b) is satisfied. Hence, the optimal policy is monotone decreasing.
